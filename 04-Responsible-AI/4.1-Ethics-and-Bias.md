## 3. Exam Focus & Key Concepts

### A. Core Principles of Responsible AI
Responsible AI is often governed by the principles of FAT:
* **Fairness:** Ensuring that AI systems do not result in harm, unequal outcomes, or discrimination toward specific groups (e.g., based on gender, race, or age).
* **Accountability:** Establishing clear ownership and governance over the entire ML lifecycle, ensuring that human experts are responsible for the AI's impact.
* **Transparency/Explainability (XAI):** Being able to understand and clearly explain *how* a model arrived at a particular decision (e.g., which input features were most important).

### B. Understanding Model Bias
Bias is systemic prejudice in an AI model or data that leads to unfair outcomes.
* **Source of Bias - Data Bias:** Bias introduced before training, resulting from the way data is collected, labeled, or sampled.
    * **Historical Bias:** Data reflects unfair outcomes from the past (e.g., loan approval rates favoring one demographic).
    * **Measurement Bias:** Inaccurate sensors or inconsistent data collection methods.
    * **Representation Bias:** The training data does not accurately reflect the real-world population (e.g., facial recognition trained mostly on light-skinned faces).
* **Mitigation Techniques:**
    * **Data Pre-processing:** Cleaning, sampling, or augmenting data to remove or reduce existing bias before training.
    * **In-processing:** Adjusting the algorithm during training.
    * **Post-processing:** Adjusting the model's output thresholds after training.

### C. The Role of Human-in-the-Loop
* **Human Review:** The use of human judgment to review confidence scores or flagged data.
* **The Supervisor (Amazon A2I):** Orchestrates human review for tasks like reviewing low-confidence bounding boxes from object detection models or checking text for toxicity. This is a critical step in managing and mitigating bias in deployed systems.
